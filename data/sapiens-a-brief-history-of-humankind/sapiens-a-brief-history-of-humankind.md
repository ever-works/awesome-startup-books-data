## Overview

From a renowned historian comes a groundbreaking narrative of humanity's creation and evolution—a #1 international bestseller—that explores the ways in which biology and history have defined us and enhanced our understanding of what it means to be "human."

One hundred thousand years ago, at least six different species of humans inhabited Earth. Yet today there is only one—homo sapiens. What happened to the others? And what may happen to us?

## Key Themes

- **The Cognitive Revolution**: How Homo sapiens came to dominate the world through the power of shared myths and stories
- **The Agricultural Revolution**: How farming changed human society forever
- **The Unification of Humankind**: The role of money, empire, and religion in creating unified cultures
- **The Scientific Revolution**: How the last 500 years transformed our species and planet

## Why Founders Should Read This

- **Understanding Shared Myths**: Companies, like empires, are built on shared beliefs and stories. Understanding how humans create and maintain these myths is crucial for founders.
- **Cooperation at Scale**: Learn how humans organize in large numbers through shared narratives—directly applicable to building companies and movements.
- **Long-term Thinking**: Gain perspective on what truly matters when building something lasting.
- **The Power of Stories**: See how narratives shape reality, essential for brand building and vision communication.
- **Pattern Recognition**: Understand historical patterns that repeat in business cycles.

## Key Insights for Business

- Money is the most universal and efficient system of mutual trust ever devised
- Large-scale human cooperation is based on shared myths and imagined orders
- The ability to create and spread stories is humanity's unique advantage
- Revolutionary changes often start with small groups thinking differently

## About the Author

Yuval Noah Harari is a historian and philosopher. Born in Israel in 1976, Harari received his PhD from the University of Oxford in 2002, and is currently a lecturer at the Department of History, the Hebrew University of Jerusalem.
