## Overview

Decisive: How to Make Better Decisions in Life and Work was published in spring 2013 and debuted at #1 on the Wall Street Journal bestseller list and #2 on the New York Times. The book is the third installment in the Heath Brothers' series on making ideas stick, creating change, and making better decisions.

## The Problem with Decisions

Research reveals that our decisions are disrupted by biases and irrationalities:
- We're overconfident in our predictions
- We seek out information that supports us and downplay information that doesn't
- We get distracted by short-term emotions
- We suffer from narrow framing, seeing too few options

Studies show that up to 83% of corporate acquisitions fail to create value, and only 17% of doctors' decisions are evidence-based.

## The WRAP Framework

The book introduces a four-step process designed to counteract decision-making biases:

### W - Widen Your Options

Avoid narrow framing by considering more alternatives:

**The Vanishing Options Test**: If your current options disappeared, what else could you do?

**Opportunity Cost**: Consider what you're giving up by choosing this option

**Find Someone Who's Solved Your Problem**: Learn from others' successes

**Ladder Your Decisions**: Break big decisions into smaller tests

**Multitrack**: Consider multiple options simultaneously rather than sequentially

### R - Reality-Test Your Assumptions

Gather trustworthy information:

**Consider the Opposite**: Ask disconfirming questions

**Zoom Out/Zoom In**: Look at base rates AND get up-close perspective

**Ooch**: Run small experiments before committing

**Ask Experts the Right Questions**: Get their best guess on probabilities, not just predictions

### A - Attain Distance Before Deciding

Overcome short-term emotion:

**10/10/10**: How will you feel about this decision in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?

**What Would You Tell Your Best Friend?**: Removes ego from the equation

**Identify and Enshrine Your Core Priorities**: Let values guide decisions

**Bookend the Future**: Prepare for both best and worst case scenarios

### P - Prepare to Be Wrong

Set up tripwires and safety nets:

**Set a Tripwire**: Decide in advance what would cause you to reconsider

**Prepare to Fail**: Have a backup plan

**Trust the Process**: Focus on good decision-making, not just good outcomes

**Create a Decision Diary**: Track your predictions to improve calibration

## Applications for Startup Founders

**Product Decisions**
- Widen options: Consider multiple feature approaches
- Reality-test: Run user tests before building
- Attain distance: Think 10/10/10 about technical debt
- Prepare to be wrong: Set metrics that would trigger a pivot

**Hiring Decisions**
- Widen options: Interview more candidates than you think necessary
- Reality-test: Check references thoroughly, ask about base rates
- Attain distance: Would you tell your best friend to hire this person?
- Prepare to be wrong: Have a clear 90-day evaluation process

**Fundraising Decisions**
- Widen options: Talk to multiple investors simultaneously
- Reality-test: Zoom out to typical deal terms, zoom in to specific investor track record
- Attain distance: How will you feel about this investor in 10 years?
- Prepare to be wrong: What metrics would trigger a down round?

**Partnership Decisions**
- Widen options: Consider multiple partnership models
- Reality-test: Talk to their other partners
- Attain distance: What would you tell a fellow founder?
- Prepare to be wrong: Build exit clauses into agreements

## Key Insights

**Ooching**: Coined by the Heaths, this means running small experiments before making big commitments. Founders should ooch whenever possible—run pilots, MVPs, and tests rather than betting everything on untested assumptions.

**The Spotlight Effect**: We overweight vivid information and underweight statistical information. Fight this by actively seeking base rates.

**Process Over Outcomes**: You can make a great decision and still have a bad outcome due to luck. Focus on improving your decision-making process.

**Short-term Emotion**: The most dangerous time to make a decision is when you're feeling strong emotions. Use the 10/10/10 rule to gain perspective.

The framework is built on research from:
- Behavioral economics (Kahneman, Tversky)
- Decision science
- Organizational behavior studies
- Chip Heath's academic research at Stanford

## Why This Book Matters for Founders

Founders make dozens of high-stakes decisions every week:
- Which features to build
- Who to hire
- How to price
- Whether to raise money
- When to pivot

Improving decision quality by even 10% can dramatically increase startup success rates.
