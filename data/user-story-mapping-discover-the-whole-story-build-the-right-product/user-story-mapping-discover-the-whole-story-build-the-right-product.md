## Overview

User Story Mapping introduces a deceptively simple approach to organizing user stories and planning product releases that keeps teams focused on users and their needs throughout development. It's the antidote to getting lost in backlogs and losing sight of what you're really trying to build.

## The Core Problem

Traditional product backlogs create several problems:
- Stories become a flat, prioritized list
- You lose sight of the big picture
- The sequence of user activities gets obscured
- Teams build features without understanding the user's journey
- Releases lack coherent narratives

User story mapping solves these problems.

## What is User Story Mapping?

A user story map is a 2-dimensional arrangement of user stories:
- **Horizontal axis**: The user's journey through your product (activities and tasks)
- **Vertical axis**: Priority and detail level
- **Result**: A visual representation that shows both the forest and the trees

## Key Concepts

### The Backbone
- User activities arranged left to right in the order users perform them
- Shows the big picture of what users are trying to accomplish
- Remains relatively stable even as details change

### Tasks and Details
- Specific user tasks arranged beneath each activity
- Details and variations stack vertically by priority
- Easy to slice horizontally for release planning

### Release Slicing
- Draw a line across the map to define a release
- Stories above the line are in scope
- Stories below the line are out of scope
- Creates a "walking skeleton" - a thin end-to-end implementation

## The Story Mapping Process

### 1. Frame the Problem
- Who are your users?
- What problems are you solving for them?
- What does success look like?

### 2. Map the Big Picture
- Describe your user's workflow at a high level
- Identify major activities (left to right)
- Focus on what users do, not what your product does

### 3. Explore
- Drill down into each activity
- Identify specific tasks and variations
- Discuss alternative approaches
- Stack details vertically

### 4. Slice Out a Release Strategy
- Draw lines defining releases
- First release: minimal viable solution (not just features)
- Subsequent releases: enhance and expand
- Each release should tell a coherent story

### 5. Slice Out a Learning Strategy
- Identify risky assumptions
- Plan experiments to validate or invalidate
- Build to learn, then build to earn

### 6. Slice Out a Development Strategy
- Break stories into technical tasks
- Plan sprints and iterations
- Keep the map visible and updated

## What Founders Will Learn

- Keeping the big picture visible throughout development
- Planning releases that deliver user value, not just features
- Involving the whole team in product planning
- Identifying and validating risky assumptions
- Breaking down MVPs into coherent user journeys
- Communicating product strategy visually
- Balancing user needs with business constraints

## Key Principles

- **Shared Understanding Beats Shared Documents**: Map together as a team
- **Tell Stories, Don't Write Requirements**: Focus on conversations
- **Solve Problems, Don't Implement Features**: Start with user needs
- **Think Through the User's Journey**: Map their experience, not your product
- **Go from Big to Small**: Start with activities, drill down to details
- **Release the Minimum Viable Solution**: Not just a list of features

## For Different Audiences

**Product Managers**: Plan and communicate product strategy visually
**Designers**: See where UX fits in the user's journey
**Developers**: Understand why you're building what you're building
**Founders**: Ensure your MVP is actually viable from a user perspective

## Common Use Cases

- Planning an MVP for a new product
- Re-platforming or rebuilding an existing product
- Understanding an existing product better
- Aligning stakeholders on product direction
- Planning complex features or workflows
- Coordinating work across multiple teams

## Why This Matters

Most startups fail because they build the wrong thing. User story mapping helps ensure you:
- Really understand your users
- Build something they'll actually use
- Can articulate why each feature matters
- Release incrementally but coherently

## Tools Needed

Basically none:
- Sticky notes on a wall (best for co-located teams)
- Or digital tools like Miro, Mural, or specialized story mapping software

The technique matters more than the tools.
