## Overview

Thinking in Bets: Making Smarter Decisions When You Don't Have All the Facts is a national bestseller by Annie Duke that offers a new way to think about decision-making under uncertainty. Duke draws on her experience as a champion poker player to teach lessons applicable to business and life.

## About the Author

Annie Duke is a former World Series of Poker champion who won more than $4 million in tournament poker before retiring to focus on writing and speaking about decision-making. She has a PhD in Cognitive Psychology from the University of Pennsylvania.

## Core Concept: Life is Poker, Not Chess

In chess, all information is visible and outcomes are determined solely by skill. In poker—and in life—we must make decisions with incomplete information and uncertainty. Good decisions can lead to bad outcomes, and bad decisions can lead to good outcomes.

## Key Principles

### Resulting

The dangerous tendency to judge decision quality by outcomes rather than by the quality of the decision process. We must:
- Separate decision quality from outcome quality
- Recognize the role of luck
- Focus on process over results

### Betting as a Decision Framework

Every decision is a bet on uncertain futures:
- What are you willing to risk?
- What are the odds?
- What's the potential payoff?
- How confident are you?

### Thinking in Probabilities

Instead of binary right/wrong thinking:
- Express beliefs as probabilities ("I'm 70% confident")
- Update beliefs based on new information
- Acknowledge uncertainty explicitly
- Make calibrated predictions

### Truth-Seeking Over Being Right

Form "decision pods" or truth-seeking groups that:
- Challenge your thinking
- Reduce motivated reasoning
- Create accountability
- Reward accurate prediction over being right

## The Buddy System

Duke advocates creating small groups committed to:
- CUDOS (Communism, Universalism, Disinterestedness, Organized Skepticism)
- Accurate belief formation
- Calling out motivated reasoning
- Sharing decision-making frameworks

## Mental Time Travel

### Backcasting
Imagine success has happened—work backward to understand what led to it.

### Premortems
Imagine failure has happened—identify what went wrong.

### 10-10-10
Consider consequences at 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years.

## Common Decision-Making Traps

### Motivated Reasoning
- Seeking information that confirms our beliefs
- Ignoring contradictory evidence
- Rationalizing after the fact

### Hindsight Bias
- "I knew it all along"
- Overestimating our predictive abilities
- Learning wrong lessons from outcomes

### Self-Serving Bias
- Attributing success to skill
- Attributing failure to bad luck
- Protecting our self-image

### Temporal Discounting
- Overvaluing immediate rewards
- Undervaluing future consequences
- Present-self vs. future-self conflicts

## Applications for Founders

### Strategic Decisions
- Assess probability of different outcomes
- Consider multiple scenarios
- Build optionality into decisions
- Update strategy based on new information

### Hiring Decisions
- Acknowledge uncertainty in candidate assessment
- Use structured processes to reduce bias
- Track prediction accuracy
- Learn from hiring outcomes

### Product Decisions
- Frame features as bets
- Define success criteria upfront
- Learn from both successes and failures
- Avoid resulting when products fail/succeed

### Fundraising
- Evaluate term sheets probabilistically
- Consider multiple paths forward
- Assess downside scenarios
- Make calibrated commitments

## Building Better Decision Groups

Characteristics of effective decision pods:
- Commitment to accuracy over loyalty
- Reward for updating beliefs
- Safe space for admitting uncertainty
- Structured feedback processes
- Diversity of perspectives

## Why It Matters for Startups

Startup founders face:
- High uncertainty
- Limited information
- Pressure for quick decisions
- High-stakes outcomes
- Cognitive biases under stress

Thinking in Bets provides a framework for making better decisions in these conditions by acknowledging uncertainty, reducing bias, and focusing on decision process rather than outcome luck.

## The Ultimate Goal

Better decisions come from:
- Acknowledging what we don't know
- Thinking probabilistically
- Separating luck from skill
- Learning from outcomes appropriately
- Building truth-seeking communities
